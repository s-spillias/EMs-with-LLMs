Below is the planned improvement with the ecological reasoning documented. We assessed that while the current model captures           
outbreak‐initiation and resource limitation well, it is missing a dynamic feedback in the predation process. In nature, when coral     
cover declines significantly, a refuge effect often occurs so that the remaining coral is less available (or provides safe havens) for 
survival. We therefore add a new parameter, refuge_effect, that reduces the effective coral predation rate when coral percentages are  
low. This directly addresses the boom–bust dynamics by ensuring that at very low coral cover, the predation losses are scaled down,    
preserving some coral and potentially moderating the outbreak collapse.

 My assessment:                                                                                                                        
 The current model captures key dynamics of COTS outbreaks and coral recovery using logistic growth, a smooth outbreak trigger, and    
 saturating resource limitation. However, one key ecological process appears oversimplified: the feedback between coral cover and      
 effective predation rates. Ecologically, when coral cover declines significantly, a refuge effect may occur where the remaining coral 
 are less accessible or provide shelter, thus reducing the effective predation rate of COTS.                                           
                                                                                                                                       
 To address this, I have added a new parameter "refuge_effect" that scales down the predation losses as coral levels drop below 100%.  
 This modification captures the reduced efficiency of COTS predation under degraded coral conditions, enhancing the model’s ability to 
 predict the timing, magnitude, and duration of outbreak events.
