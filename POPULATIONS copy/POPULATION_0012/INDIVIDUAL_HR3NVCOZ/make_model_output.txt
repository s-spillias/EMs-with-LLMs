                                                            Current Model Evaluation                                                            

Strengths:                                                                                                                                      

 1 The model includes temperature-dependent recruitment with both optimal temperature response and nutrient-mediated larval survival            
 2 Age-structured mortality (juvenile vs. adult) with temperature-dependent maturation                                                          
 3 Local spawning feedback mechanism that can sustain outbreaks                                                                                 
 4 Type II functional response for coral predation                                                                                              
 5 Multiple density-dependent processes (Allee effects, crowding, food limitation)                                                              

Potential Issues:                                                                                                                               

 1 Missing seasonal dynamics: COTS outbreaks on the GBR are strongly seasonal, with spawning occurring in summer (Dec-Feb) and recruitment      
   pulses following. The current model uses annual time steps without capturing intra-annual variation in temperature and recruitment timing.   
 2 Oversimplified nutrient proxy: The nutrient-mediated larval survival uses only SST deviation as a proxy for phytoplankton blooms. Real blooms
   depend on nutrient runoff (rainfall/river discharge) interacting with temperature, not just temperature alone.                               
 3 Linear coral recovery: Coral growth uses simple logistic growth without accounting for temperature effects on coral growth rates, which are  
   well-documented in the literature.                                                                                                           
 4 Missing predator satiation: During extreme outbreaks, COTS can become so abundant that they deplete coral faster than they can consume it    
   efficiently, leading to wasteful feeding behavior not captured by the current Type II response.                                              

                                                         Key Ecological Process to Add                                                          

Temperature-dependent coral growth rates - This is a critical missing mechanism because:                                                        

 1 Ecological justification: Coral growth rates are strongly temperature-dependent, with optimal growth at 26-28°C and reduced growth at        
   temperature extremes. This is well-documented in the literature.                                                                             
 2 Outbreak dynamics: Temperature affects both COTS (recruitment, maturation) and corals (growth, recovery). Currently, only COTS processes are 
   temperature-dependent, creating an asymmetry.                                                                                                
 3 Recovery timing: After COTS outbreaks decline, coral recovery rates determine how quickly the system can return to pre-outbreak states.      
   Temperature-dependent coral growth will affect this recovery trajectory.                                                                     
 4 Climate change relevance: As ocean temperatures rise, this mechanism becomes increasingly important for predicting future outbreak dynamics  
   and coral resilience.                                                                                                                        

                                                            Implementation Strategy                                                             

I'll add temperature-dependent coral growth using a thermal performance curve (TPC) approach:                                                   

 • Optimal growth at intermediate temperatures (26-28°C)                                                                                        
 • Reduced growth at both cooler and warmer temperatures                                                                                        
 • Separate thermal optima for fast vs. slow corals (Acropora typically prefer slightly warmer temperatures than massive corals)                

This adds ecological realism without excessive complexity, using well-established physiological principles.
