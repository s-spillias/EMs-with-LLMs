                                                              Key Issue Identified                                                              

The model currently treats coral thermal performance curves identically in mathematical form for both fast and slow corals. However, the        
literature-constrained temp_optimal_slow parameter and ecological theory suggest that slow-growing massive corals (Porites, Faviidae) have      
fundamentally different thermal tolerance breadths than fast-growing Acropora.                                                                  

Massive corals are known to be more thermally tolerant with broader performance curves, while Acropora are more thermally sensitive with        
narrower curves. The current model uses the same Gaussian form with the same sensitivity parameter structure for both, which may not capture    
this critical difference in thermal resilience.                                                                                                 

                                                              Proposed Improvement                                                              

Add differential thermal tolerance breadth between coral functional groups by allowing the thermal sensitivity parameters to vary independently 
AND adding an asymmetric thermal stress response for bleaching events (temperatures above optimal are more damaging than below).

 1 Better capturing how COTS outbreaks interact with thermal stress events                                                                      
 2 Explaining why fast corals (preferred COTS prey) may be disproportionately affected during warm periods

 +Implement ASYMMETRIC THERMAL PERFORMANCE CURVES with differential sensitivity above vs below optimal temperature:

 +- If T < T_optimal: growth_modifier = exp(-sensitivity_cool * (T - T_optimal)²)

 +                                                                                                                                              
 +Where sensitivity_warm > sensitivity_cool, creating asymmetric response.                                                                      
 +                                                                                                                                              
 +This allows:                                                                                                                                  
 +- Fast corals to have high sensitivity_warm (vulnerable to heat stress, narrow thermal tolerance)                                             
 +- Slow corals to have lower sensitivity_warm (resistant to heat stress, broad thermal tolerance)                                              
 +- Both groups to have moderate sensitivity_cool (cooling less damaging than heating)

 +                                                                                                                                              
 +1. REALISM: Coral bleaching occurs primarily during warm anomalies, not cold anomalies. Asymmetric curves capture this biological reality.

 +   - Reduce COTS food availability, increasing food limitation mortality
