Looking at the parameter values, I notice that I_opt (150 μmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹) is significantly lower than I_0 (443 μmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹). This means surface waters experience strong photoinhibition. However, the       
current model uses a single depth-averaged light value, which doesn't capture the vertical gradient where phytoplankton at different depths experience vastly different light conditions.                                   

Missing Ecological Process:                                                                                                                                                                                                 

The model oversimplifies vertical light heterogeneity. In reality, phytoplankton near the surface are photoinhibited while those deeper in the mixed layer are light-limited. The current depth-averaged approach may       
underestimate total productivity because it doesn't account for the fact that phytoplankton can optimize their position or that mixing exposes cells to varying light conditions.

Add temperature-dependent metabolic scaling for phytoplankton and zooplankton rates. Temperature is a fundamental driver of metabolic processes in marine ecosystems, affecting growth rates, grazing rates, and mortality. 
This is particularly relevant for understanding seasonal dynamics and climate change impacts on plankton populations.                                                                                                       

The improvement uses Q10 temperature coefficients (standard in marine ecology) to scale biological rates. This adds ecological realism without excessive complexity and is well-supported by empirical data showing that    
metabolic rates approximately double for every 10°C increase in temperature.
